Resistance or Reality? Validating Agent Complaints After a CRM Overhaul

Confidential client · Polish market

When agents complained a CRM update was making their work harder, the stakeholder assumed resistance to change. I proposed a usability survey instead of a satisfaction survey — and found the complaints were accurate.

175 respondents · Survey design · Quantitative analysis

Context

An extensive CRM system supports insurance agents in their daily work — from managing customer data to issuing policies. Following a significant interface update, agents began complaining that finding client information now required more clicks and tab-switching than before. The stakeholder's assumption was straightforward: users simply resist change, and frustration would fade with time.

But accepting that assumption without investigation carried real risk. If agents were experiencing genuine usability problems, further changes built on the same logic would deepen the frustration. The stakeholder wanted a survey — but one designed to confirm their assumption. Instead, I proposed reframing the research question: not "do users like the new version" but "does the new interface actually support agents in their daily work."

Methods used

Likert-scale survey Quantitative analysis Open-ended responses 175 respondents

With no behavioral tracking tools available, a survey was the most practical way to answer the primary question at scale. The survey ran 2–3 months after the changes — when initial adjustment friction had likely settled, meaning remaining difficulties were more likely to reflect genuine usability issues. The dimensions were chosen to triangulate from multiple angles: general system support, speed, data findability, tab navigation, and comprehension of the Contact and Consents area.

"Each dimension was testing a different possible explanation for the same user complaint."

Key findings

31%

of agents reported the area makes their daily work harder

29%

rated their work speed in this area as slow

76%

of those struggling cited number of clicks as the primary cause

8%

rated Contact & Consents comprehension negatively — the most changed area

The core finding: an efficiency problem, not a comprehension problem. Users weren't lost — they knew where their data was. But information that previously lived in one view had been fragmented across tabs, turning single actions into multi-step sequences. Difficulties appeared consistently regardless of seniority and sales model, ruling out the "they just need to adapt" explanation.

One finding stood out for what it didn't show. The Contact and Consents section — the most complex change — was largely fine. Only 8% rated it negatively. This redirected attention toward something structural: layout and navigation decisions affecting the entire area.

Recommendations

  • 01Identify which data points agents access most frequently and consolidate their availability — reducing tab-switching for common workflows without requiring a full redesign.
  • 02Follow up with 4–5 short observational interviews to test whether View 360 — the existing data dashboard the stakeholder cited as a solution — is actually visible and used in daily workflows.
  • ReflectionIn retrospect: a card sorting exercise would have helped prioritize which data fields mattered most before making layout decisions. And some of the friction agents experienced may have been a change management problem — changes introduced without usability validation or structured communication.

Outcome

In backlog

The findings were presented to the stakeholder. The response was skepticism — existing features were cited as counterevidence. I held my position clearly, returned the conversation to what the data showed, and accepted that the outcome wasn't mine to control. The topic was passed to the responsible team.

What I took from this: data is necessary but not sufficient. Research findings land differently depending on context, timing, and who has ownership over the decisions they challenge. The friction agents experienced wasn't inevitable — it was the predictable result of introducing significant changes without prior usability validation.

We Could See Where. We Couldn't See Why.

Usability research on a car insurance flow built for experts — used by everyone · Confidential client · Polish market

Conversion was dropping. Hotjar showed where users were leaving. What it couldn't show was why. We ran usability testing with three real users across different experience levels — and found the flow had been built for someone who already knew what they wanted. Most users didn't.

3 participants · Usability testing · User story map · Hotjar analysis

Context

A direct-to-consumer website for comparing and purchasing car insurance policies. Conversion was the primary business metric — and it wasn't performing. We had data. Conversion rates, Hotjar recordings, behavioral signals pointing toward the comparison step. We could see what was happening. Sometimes we could even see what users were looking at.

But we couldn't ask them anything. Behavioral data tells you where the problem is. It doesn't tell you why it exists. It's like watching someone through a window — you can observe, but you'll never understand without talking to them. Usability testing with just five users can surface approximately 85% of usability problems in a product.

Methods used

Hotjar analysis User story map Usability testing 3 personas

Before running sessions, we built a user story map — mapping the full purchase journey across three personas and identifying potential pain points at each stage. This wasn't just preparation. It gave us hypotheses to test: if real users hit the same friction points, findings would be more credible. Sessions were run with a structured scenario designed to surface friction without leading participants.

Participants

22F

Low experience

No insurance background · digitally fluent · tested on mobile

48F

High experience

Experienced insurance buyer · came prepared · tested on desktop

28M

Mid experience

Moderate insurance experience · technically confident · tested on mobile

All three hit the comparison step as a major friction point — but for entirely different reasons. Same step. Same problem. Three different root causes.

Key findings — 6 patterns

  • 01Technical failures at critical moments — calculation errors surfacing at purchase, scope not saved after recalculation, mobile widget crashes resetting entire flows.
  • 02Flow structure mismatched user reality — no upfront document requirements, scope selection appearing too late, no path for edge cases like new car buyers.
  • 03Comparison and decision support failures — basic sorting only, no meaningful filters by price/company/coverage. Users manually scanned every product.
  • 04Copy and comprehension problems at every stage — tooltips added more jargon, not clarity. Insurance language throughout, not plain language.
  • 05Trust failures throughout — installment options felt deceptive without total cost, recommended products unexplained. One user downloaded documents mid-session to verify they weren't empty.
  • 06Device and context mismatch — 2 of 3 users said mid-session they'd prefer to buy on desktop. The product was being used in a mobile context users weren't choosing by preference.

Recommendations

  • 01Add meaningful filtering to the comparison step — by price range, insurance company, and coverage type. Technically complex, but the highest-impact single change for conversion.
  • 02Rewrite microcopy throughout — plain language, full installment costs visible, recommended products explained, not just labeled.
  • 03Reduce form friction — eliminate unnecessary fields, pull data via API where possible, reconsider control types so inputs feel distinct and appropriate to the expected answer.
  • NoteChanges were being made without clearly defined success metrics beyond overall conversion. Metrics closer to the problem — time on comparison step, drop-off at specific points — would make it possible to evaluate whether individual changes worked before their effect on conversion became visible.

Outcome

In backlog

Most findings went into the backlog, waiting for budget. A few smaller improvements were made. The study had a known limitation: participants were recruited internally, not from the actual target audience. The findings were real. The conditions weren't ideal. Both things are true.

What I'd do differently: recruit participants from the actual user profile, establish recording infrastructure, and — most importantly — work inside a process where usability testing happens before features launch rather than after conversion drops. The research was useful. In a better-structured environment, it would have been actionable. That's the gap I want to work in next.
